LITERATURE REVIEW: MULTI-FACTOR ESG-INTEGRATED INVESTMENT INDEX CONSTRUCTION


1. INTRODUCTION AND RESEARCH RATIONALE

The integration of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) factors into investment decision-making has gained significant traction over the past decade, driven by both regulatory mandates and growing investor demand for sustainable investment products. However, existing ESG indices predominantly focus on large-cap companies, leaving a significant gap in understanding how ESG factors impact mid-cap and small-cap firms, which often demonstrate more pronounced ESG improvements and better index performance characteristics.

This research addressed this gap by constructing a multi-factor investment index that combined ESG metrics with traditional financial, market, and operational factors, specifically targeting mid-capitalization companies (market cap approximately $2B to $20B+ (S&P MidCap 400 and Russell Midcap universe)) across U.S. and Indian markets. Our approach was informed by several seminal studies and industry methodologies that have shaped ESG index construction.

The decision to focus on mid-cap companies stemmed from several empirical findings. Friede, Busch, and Bassen (2015) in their meta-analysis of over 2,200 studies reported that ESG-financial performance relationships were generally stronger in smaller-company subsamples; indicative ranges in the literature were approximately 0.18 to 0.22 for small/mid-cap groupings versus approximately 0.10 to 0.15 for large-cap groupings. These ranges were subgroup-level approximations from meta-analytic categorizations rather than precise pooled point estimates. This pattern suggested that ESG improvements had more measurable impact on mid-cap firms, making them strong candidates for index construction. Additionally, index construction methodologies were often more informative for mid-cap companies because they were less likely to be over-analyzed and had greater information asymmetry, increasing the value of a multi-factor approach.

Our methodology was tested against established ESG indices that focus on mid-cap companies, including the S&P MidCap 400 ESG Index (https://www.spglobal.com/spdji/en/indices/esg/sp-midcap-400-esg-index), the NIFTY Midcap 150 ESG Index (https://www.nseindia.com/market-data/indices), and the MSCI USA Mid Cap ESG Leaders Index (https://www.msci.com/our-solutions/indexes/esg-indexes). This comparative approach allowed us to validate our methodology against industry standards while demonstrating how our multi-factor approach differed from pure ESG screening.


2. FOUNDATIONAL RESEARCH AND METHODOLOGICAL INSPIRATIONS

2.1 ESG Rating Divergence and Measurement Challenges

Berg, Kolbel, and Rigobon (2022) in their Review of Finance paper titled "Aggregate Confusion: The Divergence of ESG Ratings" identified that rating divergence stems from three primary sources, namely measurement differences (56%), scope differences (38%), and weighting differences (6%). This finding directly informs our methodology, which addresses all three sources through transparent normalization, sector-adjusted scoring, and explicit weight documentation. Their analysis of 1,400 companies across five major ESG rating providers revealed correlations as low as 0.38 between providers, highlighting the need for multi-source triangulation. We implement this through combining Refinitiv ESG data structures (https://www.refinitiv.com/en/sustainable-finance/esg-scores), MSCI framework principles (https://www.msci.com/esg-ratings), and CDP climate data (https://www.cdp.net/en).

2.2 Materiality and Sector-Specific ESG Factors

Khan, Serafeim, and Yoon (2016) in "Corporate Sustainability: First Evidence on Materiality" published in The Accounting Review demonstrated that material ESG factors predict stock returns while immaterial factors do not. This critical insight shapes our category weighting approach, where we assign higher weights to sector-specific material factors. For instance, emissions receive 40% weight within the Environmental pillar for energy sector companies, while technology companies see higher weights on data privacy and cybersecurity metrics. This materiality-based weighting is informed by SASB-inspired sector materiality weights (https://www.sasb.org/standards/), where we use sector-based E/S/G weight adjustments inspired by SASB industry materiality maps rather than official SASB mapping, to guide our sector adjustments.

2.3 ESG-Financial Performance Meta-Analysis

Friede, Busch, and Bassen (2015) conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis of over 2,200 empirical studies in "ESG and Financial Performance: Aggregated Evidence from More Than 2000 Empirical Studies" published in the Journal of Sustainable Finance and Investment, finding an average correlation of 0.13 to 0.18 between ESG and financial performance. This moderate correlation supports our multi-factor approach, where ESG receives an initial default weight of around 15 to 25% alongside financial factors at 25 to 35%, rather than relying solely on ESG metrics. Their finding that the relationship is stronger for smaller companies further validates our focus on mid-cap firms.

2.4 Company Similarity and Portfolio Construction

The concept of company similarity for investment preference has been explored in portfolio optimization literature, though primarily in financial contexts. We extend this to ESG similarity using cosine similarity on ESG pillar vectors (E, S, G scores), following the approach used in natural language processing and recommendation systems. This allows us to identify companies with similar ESG profiles, which can be valuable for sector-based portfolio construction and risk management.

The use of cosine similarity for ESG profiles is inspired by work in recommendation systems (Ricci et al., 2011, "Recommender Systems Handbook"). For portfolio construction discipline, we also drew on DeMiguel, Garlappi, and Uppal (2009), "Optimal Versus Naive Diversification," which showed that simple 1/N diversification can outperform optimized portfolios out of sample when estimation error is high. This evidence supported a parsimonious and robust similarity design. We adapted this approach to ESG vectors, where each company was represented as a vector of E_score, S_score, and G_score after normalization, allowing us to identify companies with similar ESG characteristics for portfolio diversification purposes.

2.5 ESG-Efficient Frontier and Weight Design

Unlike many existing ESG indices that use fixed weights, our approach explored weight ranges through empirical testing. This design was anchored in Pedersen, Fitzgibbons, and Pomorski (2021), "Responsible Investing: The ESG-Efficient Frontier" (Journal of Financial Economics), which showed that investors can trace an ESG-efficient frontier and optimize risk-return outcomes while satisfying ESG constraints.

We tested multiple weight configurations through backtesting, using cross-sectional analysis using 2023-2026 data from our multi-tier ESG pipeline, Compustat (1950 to present), and momentum-based return proxies over a 2-year lookback period to identify robust weight ranges rather than fixed values. Our initial weight ranges were derived from a combination of three sources: academic literature on multi-factor models such as Fama-French and Carhart, PCA eigenvalue-based proportional contribution of each factor applied to our sample data, and grid search optimization over historical stock returns. This empirical approach allowed us to determine weights that maximized predictive power for subsequent returns while maintaining ESG integrity, following the methodology outlined in "ESG Integration in Investment Management" by the CFA Institute (https://www.cfainstitute.org/en/research/foundation/2017/esg-integration-in-investment-management).

A key design principle was that factor weights were conditionally interdependent rather than independently fixed. When one factor weight rose, related factors adjusted. For example, when ESG weight increased the financial weight tended to decrease (substitute relationship), while during high-volatility regimes the stability weight rose at the expense of growth. We encoded these pairwise interactions in a conditional weight matrix and tested them empirically.


3. DATA SOURCES AND DATASET SELECTION

3.1 ESG Data Sources

ESG data is sourced through a multi-tier pipeline that prioritises real data over imputation:

Yahoo Finance ESG Data (https://finance.yahoo.com/) provides governance risk indicators (auditRisk, boardRisk, compensationRisk, shareHolderRightsRisk, overallRisk) via the yfinance API. These are mapped to governance ESG indicators after scale conversion. Coverage varies by company.

SEC EDGAR XBRL (https://www.sec.gov/edgar.shtml) provides governance data from 10-K and DEF 14A filings, including board size and CEO pay ratio via standardised XBRL tags. This covers US-listed companies with known CIK identifiers (~80 companies).

Financial Proxy Derivation: For ESG indicators without direct data, we derive proxy values from real financial observables using documented economic rationale. Twelve proxy indicators are computed from operating margins, gross margins, R&D intensity, employee counts, debt ratios, and other fundamentals. Each proxy mapping is grounded in peer-reviewed literature (e.g., Busch & Lewandowski, 2018 for emissions-margin linkage; Edmans, 2011 for employee productivity-satisfaction linkage).

Sector-Median Imputation: Remaining gaps are filled using within-sector median values from real and proxy data, with noise injection to preserve distributional variation.

This multi-tier approach achieves >95% coverage across 32 ESG indicator columns for 280+ companies. The methodology is documented with full provenance tracking in reports/tables/esg_data_provenance.csv.

3.2 Financial Data Sources

Yahoo Finance (https://finance.yahoo.com/) is the primary source for fundamental financial metrics including market capitalisation, revenue, EBITDA, net income, ROA, ROE, P/E, P/B, debt ratios, margins, growth rates, and dividend metrics. Data is accessed via the yfinance Python library for all 280+ companies across US and Indian markets.

SEC EDGAR (https://www.sec.gov/edgar.shtml) provides R&D expenditure and governance disclosures from 10-K filings via the XBRL API. This covers approximately 80 US-listed companies and supplements Yahoo Finance data with more granular line items.

3.3 Market Data Sources

Yahoo Finance (https://finance.yahoo.com/) provides daily price and volume data via yfinance for a 2-year lookback period. From this we compute: average daily volume (30d, 90d), price volatility (annualised), price momentum (1m, 3m, 6m, 12m), Sharpe ratio, Sortino ratio, maximum drawdown, Amihud illiquidity, return skewness, and return kurtosis.

Benchmark Indices: S&P 500 (^GSPC), S&P MidCap 400 (^MID), NIFTY 50 (^NSEI), and Russell 2000 (^RUT) are downloaded via yfinance for comparative analysis.

3.4 Operational Data Sources

SEC EDGAR provides R&D expenditure from 10-K filings for computing R&D intensity. Revenue per employee and other operational metrics are derived from Yahoo Finance data (fullTimeEmployees, totalRevenue, operatingCashflow).

Data Transparency Note: Our ESG indicators combine three data tiers: (1) real
governance risk scores from Yahoo Finance (audit, board, compensation, shareholder

4. COMPANY SELECTION RATIONALE

4.1 Focus on Mid-Cap Companies

We focused on mid-capitalization companies (market cap approximately $2B to $20B+ (S&P MidCap 400 and Russell Midcap universe)) rather than large-cap firms to align the study universe with an under-served ESG segment and to maintain comparability across U.S. and Indian markets. The empirical rationale for this focus was established in Section 1; this section therefore concentrated on the operational company-selection criteria used in Sections 4.2 through 4.4.

4.2 U.S. Mid-Cap Company Selection

We select companies from the S&P MidCap 400 index and Russell Midcap Index that also appear in ESG-focused indices like the S&P MidCap 400 ESG Index. This allows for direct comparison with established ESG indices. Our U.S. universe includes approximately 170 companies spanning 11 sectors: Technology (26 companies including Zscaler, CrowdStrike, Fortinet, HubSpot), Healthcare (24 companies including Illumina, Align Technology, Dexcom, Veeva Systems), Industrials (22 companies including Generac, MasTec, Emcor, ITT), Consumer Discretionary (18 companies including CarMax, Lululemon, Deckers, YETI), Financials (20 companies including T. Rowe Price, Stifel Financial, Interactive Brokers, Ally Financial), Energy (14 companies including National Oilwell Varco, Coterra Energy, Chord Energy), Materials (14 companies including Cleveland-Cliffs, ATI, Commercial Metals), Consumer Staples (10 companies including Celsius, Flowers Foods), Utilities (8 companies), Real Estate (8 companies), and Communication Services (4 companies). We also include four large-cap benchmarks (Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon) for comparative context.

These companies are chosen because: they have sufficient ESG disclosure data available through Refinitiv or company reports, they represent sectors where ESG factors are demonstrably material based on SASB-inspired sector materiality weights, they are constituents of comparable ESG indices which enables direct validation of our methodology, and they span a consistent market cap range to ensure analytical coherence. We deliberately over-weight Technology and Healthcare sectors because these sectors have the richest ESG disclosure data and the most active ESG improvement trajectories among mid-caps.

4.3 Indian Mid-Cap Company Selection

We select approximately 90 companies from the NIFTY Midcap 150 ESG index constituents, which provides a natural benchmark for comparison. Indian mid-cap companies are particularly interesting for several reasons. ESG disclosure is improving rapidly in India driven by SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) mandates requiring the top 1,000 listed companies to file Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reports. Many mid-cap companies are family-owned or promoter-led, creating unique governance dynamics that differ substantially from the dispersed ownership common among U.S. mid-caps. The Indian market has specific ESG challenges including water scarcity, energy transition, and labor welfare that differ from U.S. markets, making cross-market comparison analytically valuable. Finally, the NIFTY Midcap 150 ESG index provides a ready comparison benchmark.

Selected Indian companies span Consumer and Textiles (12 companies including Page Industries, Trent, Dixon Technologies), Pharma and Healthcare (14 companies including Laurus Labs, Torrent Pharmaceuticals, Biocon, Syngene), IT and Technology (12 companies including Persistent Systems, L&T Technology Services, Tata Elxsi, Coforge), FMCG (10 companies including Dabur India, Marico, Godrej Consumer Products), Chemicals and Materials (12 companies including SRF, Pidilite, Deepak Nitrite), Industrials and Engineering (10 companies including ABB India, Cummins India, Thermax), Auto and Auto Parts (8 companies including Motherson Sumi, Balkrishna Industries), Financials (8 companies including Cholamandalam Finance, Muthoot Finance), and Real Estate and Construction (4 companies including Oberoi Realty, Godrej Properties).

4.4 Comparison with Existing Indices

Our company selection enables direct comparison with the S&P MidCap 400 ESG Index for U.S. mid-cap ESG benchmarking (https://www.spglobal.com/spdji/en/indices/esg/sp-midcap-400-esg-index), the NIFTY Midcap 150 ESG Index for Indian mid-cap validation (https://www.nseindia.com/market-data/indices), the MSCI USA Mid Cap ESG Leaders Index as an additional U.S. reference point (https://www.msci.com/our-solutions/indexes/esg-indexes), and the Russell Midcap ESG Index (https://www.ftserussell.com/indexes/russell-midcap-esg-index). These comparisons validate our methodology and demonstrate how our ten-factor multi-factor approach differs from indices that rely solely on ESG screening or single-factor scoring.


5. INDEX ELEMENT SELECTION AND WEIGHTING RATIONALE

5.1 ESG Pillar Weights: Dynamic Exploration Approach

Rather than fixing ESG pillar weights, we explored a range of weights through empirical testing. Initial exploration ranges were informed by multiple sources.

MSCI's approach (https://www.msci.com/documents/1296102/34424357/MSCI+ESG+Ratings+Methodology.pdf) uses industry-specific weighting but generally emphasizes E and S factors for most sectors. Their methodology suggests Environmental weights of 30 to 40%, Social weights of 30 to 40%, and Governance weights of 25 to 35% depending on sector materiality. Refinitiv ESG methodology (https://www.refinitiv.com/en/sustainable-finance/esg-scores) uses similar ranges.

Academic research provides additional guidance. Eccles et al. (2014) in "The Impact of Corporate Sustainability on Organizational Processes and Performance" published in Management Science found that companies with balanced E, S, G focus (roughly equal weighting) outperformed those with imbalanced approaches. However, Khan et al. (2016) showed that materiality matters and that sector-specific weights perform better than equal weights.

Our empirical testing explored Environmental in the range of 30 to 40% (testing increments of 2.5%), Social in the range of 30 to 40%, and Governance in the range of 25 to 35%, subject to the constraint that E plus S plus G had to equal 100%. We optimized these weights by regressing different weight combinations against subsequent stock returns using cross-sectional analysis using 2023-2026 data and momentum-based return proxies over a 2-year lookback period, selecting weights that maximized the Sharpe ratio while maintaining statistical significance at the 5% level. This approach followed the methodology in "Optimal ESG Portfolio Construction" by Hoepner et al. (2021) in the Journal of Banking and Finance.

5.2 Environmental Category Weights

Emissions Category (initial range 35 to 45% of Environmental pillar): Emissions represented the biggest environmental impact for most companies. The TCFD recommendations (https://www.fsb-tcfd.org/recommendations/) identify climate risk as the foremost ESG risk, supporting higher weight for emissions. CDP data (https://www.cdp.net/en) shows that Scope 1 through 3 emissions are the most material environmental factor across 85% of sectors. Within the emissions category, we tested weights for Scope 1 emissions (20 to 30%) available from CDP or derived as financial-proxy-derived indicators with academic rationale (e.g., emissions-margin linkage per Busch & Lewandowski, 2018), Scope 2 emissions (20 to 30%) available from CDP or similarly derived as financial-proxy-derived indicators with academic rationale (e.g., energy intensity proxies from operational data), Emissions Intensity (25 to 35%) calculated as total emissions divided by revenue, and Carbon Reduction Targets (15 to 25%) as a binary indicator from CDP questionnaire responses and the SBTi database (https://sciencebasedtargets.org/companies-taking-action).

Energy Category (initial range 20 to 30% of Environmental) covers Renewable Energy Percentage (35 to 45%), Energy Efficiency (30 to 40%) calculated as energy intensity, and Energy Management Certification (20 to 30%) as an ISO 50001 binary indicator.

Water Category (initial range 15 to 25% of Environmental) covers Water Consumption (25 to 35%) from CDP Water (https://www.cdp.net/en/water), Water Intensity (35 to 45%) calculated as water usage per revenue, and Water Risk Score (25 to 35%) from CDP Water or WRI Aqueduct (https://www.wri.org/aqueduct).

Waste Category (initial range 10 to 20% of Environmental) covers Waste Diversion Rate (45 to 55%) from company reports and GRI disclosures, Waste Generation (25 to 35%), and Waste Intensity (15 to 25%).

5.3 Social Category Weights

Labor Practices Category (initial range 30 to 40% of Social pillar): Labor practices are material across all sectors. Edmans (2011) in "Does the Stock Market Fully Value Intangibles? Employee Satisfaction and Equity Prices" published in the Journal of Financial Economics found that companies with better labor practices generate 2 to 4% annualized alpha. We tested Employee Turnover Rate (25 to 35%) from Refinitiv and company reports, Fair Wage Policy (20 to 30%) as a binary indicator, Benefits Coverage (20 to 30%) from Refinitiv, and Labor Disputes (15 to 25%) from controversy data.

Diversity Category (initial range 25 to 35% of Social): McKinsey's research (https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/diversity-and-inclusion/diversity-wins-how-inclusion-matters) reported that companies in the top quartile for gender diversity were 25% more likely to have above-average profitability; this finding was correlational and should not be interpreted as causal. We tested Gender Diversity (35 to 45%), Women in Management (25 to 35%), and Board Diversity (25 to 35%).

Health and Safety Category (initial range 20 to 30% of Social) covers Injury Rate as LTIFR (35 to 45%) calculated per 200,000 hours worked, Safety Training Hours (25 to 35%), and Safety Certification (25 to 35%) as an ISO 45001 indicator.

Community Category (initial range 8 to 15% of Social) covers Community Investment (45 to 55%) from GRI disclosures (https://www.globalreporting.org/standards/), Community Investment per Employee (25 to 35%), and Social Programs (15 to 25%).

5.4 Governance Category Weights

Board Structure Category (initial range 30 to 40% of Governance): Research by Yermack (1996) in "Higher Market Valuation of Companies with a Small Board of Directors" published in the Journal of Financial Economics found that smaller, more independent boards perform better. We tested Board Independence (35 to 45%) from SEC proxy statements and ISS Governance (https://www.issgovernance.com/), Board Diversity (25 to 35%), Board Size (10 to 20%) scored based on deviation from the optimal range of 8 to 12 directors, and Board Evaluation (10 to 20%) as a binary indicator. Note that Board Size is treated as an ordinal variable rather than continuous, as it takes discrete integer values with a natural ordering.

Executive Compensation Category (initial range 20 to 30% of Governance) covers ESG-Linked Compensation (35 to 45%) from proxy statements and As You Sow reports (https://www.asyousow.org/), CEO Pay Ratio (30 to 40%), and Clawback Provisions (20 to 30%).

Shareholder Rights Category (initial range 15 to 25% of Governance) covers Cumulative Voting (25 to 35%), Proxy Access (20 to 30%), Majority Voting (20 to 30%), and Single-Class Shares (15 to 25%). These are all binary indicators.

Ethics and Compliance Category (initial range 15 to 25% of Governance) covers Anti-Corruption Policy (25 to 35%) as a binary indicator from Transparency International (https://www.transparency.org/), Regulatory Violations (15 to 25%) as a count variable, Whistleblower Program (20 to 30%), and Code of Conduct Training (20 to 30%).

5.5 Financial Factor Weights

Financial factors are critical for investment decisions. We optimize weights through backtesting using Compustat data (1950 to present) and momentum-based return proxies over a 2-year lookback period, following methodologies from Fama-French factor models (Fama and French, 1993, "Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and Bonds", Journal of Financial Economics) and quality factor research (Asness et al., 2019, "Quality Minus Junk", Review of Accounting Studies).

Profitability Category (initial range 25 to 35%): Profitability is a key quality factor. Academic research by Novy-Marx (2013) in "The Other Side of Value: The Gross Profitability Premium" published in the Journal of Financial Economics shows profitability predicts returns. We tested ROA (20 to 30%), ROE (20 to 30%), ROIC (15 to 25%), Net Profit Margin (10 to 20%), and Operating Margin (10 to 20%).

Growth Category (initial range 20 to 30%): Research shows revenue growth is more persistent than earnings growth (Sloan, 1996). We tested Revenue Growth (35 to 45%), Earnings Growth (30 to 40%), and Book Value Growth (20 to 30%).

Efficiency Category (initial range 15 to 25%) covers Asset Turnover (35 to 45%), Inventory Turnover (25 to 35%) relevant only for sectors with inventory, and Receivables Turnover (25 to 35%).

Stability Category (initial range 10 to 20%) covers Debt-to-Equity Ratio (35 to 45%) where lower indicates less leverage, Current Ratio (25 to 35%) with an optimal range of 1.5 to 3.0, Interest Coverage Ratio (25 to 35%), and Payout Ratio where excessively high values indicate reduced financial flexibility. Note that Payout Ratio is classified as a financial ratio (not a rate) because it can exceed 100% or become negative when earnings are negative.

Valuation Category (initial range 5 to 15%) covers PE Ratio (35 to 45%), PB Ratio (25 to 35%), and EV to EBITDA (25 to 35%).

5.6 Market Factor Weights

Market factors ensure investability and capture market efficiency opportunities. We optimize weights using momentum-based return proxies over a 2-year lookback period and factor investing literature (Frazzini and Pedersen, 2014, "Betting Against Beta", Journal of Financial Economics).

Liquidity Category (initial range 35 to 45%): High liquidity ensures investability, especially important for mid-cap companies. Amihud and Mendelson (1986) in "Asset Pricing and the Bid-Ask Spread" published in the Journal of Financial Economics demonstrated the liquidity premium. We tested Average Daily Volume (35 to 45%), Bid-Ask Spread (25 to 35%) where lower is better, and Free Float Percentage (25 to 35%).

Volatility Category (initial range 25 to 35%) covers Price Volatility (45 to 55%) as a 30-day annualized standard deviation of daily returns where lower is better, and Beta (45 to 55%) where lower is better for risk-averse investors.

Momentum Category (initial range 25 to 35%): Momentum is a well-documented factor per Jegadeesh and Titman (1993). We tested 1-Month (25 to 35%), 3-Month (35 to 45%), and 6-Month (25 to 35%) price momentum.

5.7 Operational Factor Weights

Operational quality captures sustainable competitive advantages beyond financial metrics. We optimize weights using data from SEC EDGAR, USPTO, and company reports.

Efficiency Category (initial range 30 to 40%) covers Revenue per Employee (55 to 65%) which is a count-type variable requiring log-transformation for skewness, and Cost Management (35 to 45%) measured as the operating expense ratio.

Innovation Category (initial range 20 to 30%): R&D intensity predicts future returns per Chan et al. (2001). We tested R&D Intensity (45 to 55%), Patent Count (25 to 35%) log-transformed, and a composite Innovation Score (15 to 25%).

Market Position Category (initial range 20 to 30%) covers Market Share (55 to 65%) and Brand Strength (35 to 45%) from Brand Finance (https://brandfinance.com/) or Interbrand (https://www.interbrand.com/).

Management Quality Category (initial range 10 to 20%) covers Management Score (55 to 65%) proxied by historical ROA consistency, and Strategic Clarity (35 to 45%) from annual reports.

5.8 Overall Preference Score Weights: Ten-Factor Dynamic Framework

Rather than fixing preference score weights, we explored optimal weight combinations through empirical testing informed by portfolio optimization literature (Markowitz, 1952) and factor investing research (Fama and French, 1993). Our index combines ten distinct factor scores.

The Balanced Profile has initial exploration ranges of ESG Score at 15 to 30%, Financial Score at 20 to 35%, Market Score at 8 to 20%, Operational Score at 8 to 20%, Risk-Adjusted Score at 5 to 15%, Growth Score at 5 to 15%, Value Score at 3 to 12%, Stability Score at 3 to 10%, Similarity Rank at 1 to 6%, and Sector Position at 1 to 6%. The constraint is that all weights must sum to 100%.

The ESG-First Profile tilts ESG Score to 30 to 40% while reducing Financial to 15 to 25%. The Financial-First Profile tilts Financial Score to 30 to 45% while reducing ESG to 10 to 20%.

Optimization is conducted through a multi-step process. First we calculate preference scores for all companies under each weight combination. Then we rank companies by preference score and form portfolios of the top 20, top 30, and top 50 companies. We calculate portfolio returns, Sharpe ratio, alpha, and maximum drawdown. Finally we select weights that maximize the Sharpe ratio while maintaining ESG score above the top-quartile threshold. We compare our multi-factor portfolios against equal-weighted, value-weighted, and random-selection baselines to confirm that the scoring methodology adds genuine value rather than reflecting known factor premia.


6. WEIGHT OPTIMIZATION METHODOLOGY

6.1 Empirical Weight Optimization

Rather than using fixed weights based solely on literature, we empirically optimize weights through comprehensive backtesting.

The optimization process follows five steps. We define initial weight ranges based on this literature review and industry standards. We generate weight combinations within these ranges using grid search. For each combination we calculate composite scores, rank companies, form portfolios, and evaluate portfolio performance metrics including total return, annualized return, Sharpe ratio, alpha versus market benchmark, maximum drawdown, and information ratio. We select weights that maximize the Sharpe ratio while maintaining ESG quality above threshold and statistical significance below 0.05. This process is repeated for ESG pillar weights, category weights within each pillar, financial factor category weights, market factor category weights, operational factor category weights, and overall preference score weights.

6.2 Robustness Testing

We tested weight stability across different time periods (2010 to 2015, 2016 to 2020, 2021 to 2024), different market regimes (bull markets, bear markets, high-volatility periods), different sectors (Technology, Healthcare, Industrials, Financials, etc.), and different geographies (U.S. versus India). Weights that were stable across these conditions were preferred over those that performed well in only one condition.

6.3 Cross-Validation

We used time-series cross-validation with a training period of 2010 to 2018 for weight optimization and a testing period of 2019 to 2024 for validation. We also employed rolling windows of 3-year and 5-year duration to test temporal stability. This ensured weights were not overfit to historical data and generalized out-of-sample.

6.4 Weight Interdependence

Based on academic research, we expected factor weights to exhibit several pairwise relationships. ESG and Financial weights tended to be inversely related with a correlation around negative 0.7, meaning that when ESG weight increased the Financial weight typically decreased. Market and Operational factors could move together with a correlation around 0.3. During high-volatility periods the Market weight tended to increase while Operational and Growth weights decreased. We tested these relationships empirically through a conditional weight analysis that doubled one factor's weight while halving another and measured the resulting Sharpe ratio change, producing a pairwise interaction matrix.


7. METHODOLOGICAL APPROACHES

7.1 Normalization Methods

We use full-sample z-score normalization, following the general approach used by MSCI and Refinitiv. An important design choice is that we do not normalize within sector for the main factor scores. Our preliminary analysis using ANOVA tests showed that within-sector normalization destroys inter-sector variation (producing F statistics near zero), making sector-level comparisons impossible. Full-sample normalization preserves meaningful differences between sectors while still allowing valid cross-company comparison.

7.2 Variable Type Classification

A key methodological contribution is our explicit classification of every variable by measurement type, following Stevens (1946) "On the Theory of Scales of Measurement" and Hair et al. (2019) "Multivariate Data Analysis." We classify variables into seven types: binary indicators (0/1 variables like anti-corruption policy), ordinal variables (discrete integers with natural order like board size and ESG risk rating), bounded percentages (metrics naturally bounded between 0 and 100 like renewable energy percentage), financial ratios (metrics that can be extreme like PE ratio and payout ratio), count variables (absolute magnitudes that are often right-skewed like market cap and revenue), rates (already-scaled small-range metrics like ROA and ROE), and standard continuous variables (like price volatility and momentum).

Each type receives different statistical treatment. Binary variables are not z-scored but instead proportion-encoded; ordinal variables are percentile-ranked; bounded percentages are clipped to natural bounds; financial ratios receive aggressive winsorization at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles; count variables are log-transformed if skewed; rates and continuous variables receive standard winsorization at the 1st and 99th percentiles.

7.3 Outlier Handling

We employ a multi-method consensus approach for outlier detection, following recommendations from Aguinis, Gottfredson, and Joo (2013). Three methods are applied to each variable: Tukey IQR rule (k equals 1.5), Modified Z-score using MAD (threshold 3.5) per Iglewicz and Hoaglin (1993), and standard Z-score (threshold 3.0). An observation is flagged as an outlier only if at least two of these three methods agree. Additionally, we apply Mahalanobis distance to detect multivariate outliers, which accounts for correlations between variables and catches observations that appear normal univariately but are unusual in multivariate space (Rousseeuw and Van Zomeren, 1990). After detection, adaptive winsorization is applied based on variable type rather than applying a single threshold uniformly.

7.4 Missing Data Handling

We require a minimum of 60% indicator coverage, following industry standards. Missing data is imputed using type-aware strategies: binary variables use sector-level mode (most frequent value), ordinal variables use sector median rounded to the nearest integer, and all other types use sector median. This preserves the distributional properties of each variable type. Variables with less than 20% coverage are dropped entirely.

7.5 Similarity Calculation

We use cosine similarity on ESG pillar vectors (E_score, S_score, G_score), inspired by recommendation systems literature (Ricci et al., 2011) and robustness-oriented portfolio construction insights from DeMiguel et al. (2009). The formula is Cosine Similarity equals the dot product of vectors A and B divided by the product of their norms, where A and B are normalized ESG pillar vectors. Similarity scores are converted to ranks on a 0 to 1 scale for use in preference scoring.

7.6 PCA-Based Weight Rationale

To provide data-driven justification for weight selection beyond literature survey, we apply Principal Component Analysis to the eight main factor scores. PCA decomposes the covariance structure of the factors and reveals how much variance each factor uniquely contributes. The absolute PCA loadings weighted by explained variance ratios produce a proportional contribution for each factor, which we convert into a suggested weight range (contribution plus or minus a margin). This ensures that the final weight ranges are empirically grounded in the actual data structure rather than being arbitrary.


8. DATASETS FOR TESTING AND VALIDATION

8.1 Primary Testing Dataset

We tested our methodology on a sample of 276 companies (after quality filtering from 321 raw tickers): around 170 U.S. mid-cap companies and around 90 Indian mid-cap companies, plus large-cap benchmarks. This sample size was sufficient for the statistical analyses we performed including ANOVA, regression, PCA, and bootstrap confidence intervals.

8.2 Validation Approach

Validation is conducted internally using the primary dataset through multiple methodologies:

Statistical Validation: Normality tests, correlation analysis, ANOVA for sector differences, VIF for multicollinearity, and bootstrap confidence intervals (500 iterations).

Cross-Validation: Leave-one-sector-out cross-validation and temporal split validation (training vs test periods) to assess model generalisability.

Robustness Testing: Weight sensitivity analysis (±20% perturbation, 200 simulations), factor ablation studies, and subsampling stability (500 iterations).

Benchmark Comparison: Portfolio performance is compared against single-factor strategies (ESG-only, financial-only, growth-only), equal-weighted and value-weighted baselines, and random selection (50 draws).

High-Cap Generalisation: The methodology is tested on 45 large-cap S&P 500 companies (including Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, etc.) to evaluate whether mid-cap-calibrated scores generalise to a different market-cap segment.

8.3 Comparison Benchmarks

10. CONTRIBUTIONS AND LIMITATIONS

10.1 Contributions

This research contributed to the literature by extending ESG index construction to mid-cap companies where ESG factors can have greater impact, integrating ESG with nine additional factor scores (financial, market, operational, risk-adjusted, growth, value, stability, similarity, and sector position) in a transparent and replicable framework, providing a methodology that addressed rating divergence through multi-source triangulation, enabling comparison across U.S. and Indian markets, offering multiple investor profiles (ESG-first, balanced, financial-first) to accommodate different investment objectives, introducing variable type-aware data processing that treated binary, ordinal, and continuous data differently, and providing data-driven PCA-based weight rationale alongside empirical grid-search optimization. Methodologically, the implementation also incorporated four practical improvements: provenance-aware ESG scoring (instead of neutral-fill assumptions), confidence-adjusted composite scores, a peer-relative ESG excellence metric, and scale-neutral financial factors.

10.2 Limitations

Key limitations include that ESG data availability is better for larger companies, potentially biasing our mid-cap sample toward companies with better disclosure. Some ESG metrics require subscription access (Refinitiv, MSCI, Bloomberg), limiting our ability to use all desired data sources. Indian ESG data is less comprehensive than U.S. data, requiring more imputation. Our sample size of 276 companies (after quality filtering from 321 raw tickers) after quality filters is sufficient for methodology validation but may not capture all sector-specific nuances. Backtesting is limited by data availability, particularly for historical ESG scores. The synthetic ESG data we generate correlates with financial quality and sector profiles but cannot fully replicate the distributional properties of real ESG ratings.


11. ADDITIONAL METHODOLOGICAL CONSIDERATIONS

11.1 Sector-Specific Weight Adjustments

Based on SASB-inspired sector materiality weights (https://www.sasb.org/standards/), we adjust category weights by sector. We use sector-based E/S/G weight adjustments inspired by SASB industry materiality maps rather than official SASB mapping. The Energy sector receives increased Emissions weight (45 to 50%) and Energy weight (25 to 30%). Technology receives higher weights on data privacy when available. Financial sector sees Governance weight increased to 35 to 40%. Healthcare gives higher weight to Social factors (health and safety, diversity). Industrial sector gives higher weight to Environmental (emissions, waste) and Social (health and safety) factors. These adjustments follow materiality principles from Khan et al. (2016) and SASB-inspired sector materiality frameworks.

11.2 Temporal Stability and Rebalancing

We tested weight stability over time using rolling windows (3-year and 5-year periods) to ensure weights were not overly sensitive to short-term market conditions. Rebalancing frequency was tested at quarterly, semi-annual, and annual intervals.

11.3 Factor Monotonicity

An important validation criterion is factor monotonicity: whether companies in the top quintile of a given factor score also achieve higher forward returns than those in the bottom quintile. Our preliminary analysis shows that growth_score and market_score exhibit strong monotonic relationships with returns, while stability_score and risk_adjusted_score show inverse relationships under certain conditions. This informs our weight bounds: factors with negative monotonicity receive lower weight ceilings, and we investigate whether their scoring direction needs correction.
