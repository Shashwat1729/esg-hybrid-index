\chapter{Results and Analysis}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter trims the story down to what really matters.
The revised index is built on 276 mid-cap firms from the US and India, and the results are mixed in a pretty beliveable way becuase the signals do not move neatly together.
Some parts look strong, some are only okay, and one limitation keeps showing up: the ESG layer is still mostly synthetic.
So yes, the index works, but not in every way we first hoped.

\section{Factor structure and sample patterns}
\label{sec:descriptive}
\label{sec:correlations}
\label{sec:sector_geo}

After re-scaling the factor space looks tidy enough.
Most scores sit almost exactly where they should, around a mean of 50 with standard deviations close to 10.
ESG\_composite comes in at 50.05 with a standard deviation of 10.09, risk\_adjusted\_score at 49.82 with 9.96, value\_score at 50.17 with 9.95, and stability\_score at 49.91 with 9.95.
The obvious outlier is financial\_score at 48.63 with a standard deviation of just 1.40, which makes cross-firm separation much tighter than expected.
That gap is a bit wierd, but it does not break the model.

The dependence checks are reassuring.
Maximum VIF is 2.80, so multicollinearity stays low, and PCA still needs three components to explain 67.4\% of total variance.
That means no single hidden axis is doing all the work.
The sample itself is broad enough to make that matter: 186 US firms, 90 Indian firms, and 11 sectors.
Technology and Healthcare generally look better on ESG and growth, while Energy and Basic Materials stay stronger on financial and risk screens.
Nothing shocking there, but it does make the final ranking easier to intepret.

\section{What the ranking actually does}
\label{sec:esg_financial}
\label{sec:factor_validity}
\label{sec:monotonicity}
\label{sec:rankings}
\label{sec:profiles}

The cleanest claim here is not ``high ESG means high returns.''
That would be too neat.
The ESG-financial fit is fairly high at $R^2 = 0.544$, but this still reads partly as a proxy-construction artefact rather than a clean discovery from observed market data.
I do not think the thesis should pretend otherwise.

What works better is the ranking logic.
Against the forward-quality target, 7 of 8 factors show monotonic ordering.
Against the return proxy, only 3 of 9 factors pass Jonckheere-Terpstra testing.
So the model is better at sorting firms by underlying quality than at calling short-horizon performance.
That is less flashy, but honestly more plausible.

The portfolio numbers tell the same story.
The balanced top-20 portfolio posts a weak $-4.01\%$ with cross-sectional IR $=-0.710$, while the financial-first top-20 does a bit better at $-3.13\%$ with IR $=0.251$.
The best construction is the score-weighted version at $+8.66\%$ with cross-sectional IR $=0.475$.
So construction choice matters a lot.
Equal-weighting looked fine at first glance, then it really did not.

At the top of teh balanced ranking, INCY, MEDP, and EXEL lead the list.
That is not a random mix.
It reflects strong growth and risk-adjusted readings more than one lucky spike.
The three investor profiles also do something real instead of just changing labels: ESG-First and Financial-First still share a core investable set, but their rank agreement is only Kendall $\tau = 0.593$.
That feels about right.

\section{Robustness and caveats}
\label{sec:robustness}
\label{sec:esg_provenance}
\label{sec:cross-provider}
\label{sec:financial_validation}
\label{sec:multiple_testing}

This is where the chapter gets more convincing.
Bootstrap ordering is extremely stable at Kendall $\tau = 1.000$, even if the rank confidence bands are still wide at 247 to 255 places.
Weight perturbations are also very tame, with profile rankings staying above Spearman $\rho = 0.99$ under \(\pm 20\%\) changes.
The balanced strategy is especially usefull in ugly markets, posting bear-period excess of $+7.27$ percentage points, or $+10.65$ percentage points in the decomposition with portfolio $\beta = 0.82$ and IR $=0.585$.
Across rolling rebalances it beats the universe in all 12 periods, with mean excess of $+7.38\%$.
That is probably the most practical result in the chapter.

The transfer test is also stronger than I expected.
On the 45-company high-cap sample, all 9 scoring dimensions generalise, with Kendall $W = 0.933$, Spearman $\rho = 0.9993$, and Friedman $p = 0.50$.
Put simply, the rank logic barely changes when the universe gets bigger and more liquid.
All six financial validation checks pass too.
So the weaker predictive bits do not seem to come from obvious accounting or pipeline mistakes.

The caveat is still the ESG input layer.
About 84.7\% of ESG observations are synthetic, 11.7\% are real Yahoo governance fields, and 3.6\% are imputed.
Cross-provider validation stays weak and underpowered in the tiny overlap sample, so I would not oversell alignment with MSCI or Sustainalytics.
Statistical correction tells a similar story: out of 521 hypothesis tests, 235 survive BH-FDR and 169 survive Bonferroni.
That is not bad, but it does trim away some of the prettier claims.

Taken together the picture is pretty clear.
The index is structurally sound, the ranking engine is usefull, and the portfolio results get a lot better once construction is handled sensibly.
But the ESG-financial story is still doing some work through proxy design, so any big causal claim would be pushing it.
That trade-off is annoying, but it is also real.
